About Algeria

Located in North Africa, it shares borders with Tunisia, Libya, Niger, Mali, Mauritania, Western Sahara, and Morocco, while also enjoying a long Mediterranean coastline. Much of the country is dominated by the mighty Sahara Desert, which covers nearly 80% of its territory. The northern coastal plain and the Atlas Mountains, however, provide fertile land for agriculture and are home to most of the population.

The history of Algeria is long and complex. Ancient Berber tribes were its earliest inhabitants, followed by the influence of Phoenicians, Romans, Byzantines, and later Arab-Muslim conquests in the 7th century, which introduced Islam and Arabic culture. In the 16th century, Algeria became part of the Ottoman Empire before falling under French colonial rule in 1830. Today, Algeria is a presidential republic where politics have often been influenced by its liberation history and powerful military. The country has witnessed social movements such as the 2019 Hirak protests, which demanded democratic reforms and political transparency. Despite challenges, Algeria plays a vital role in the African Union, Arab League, and Mediterranean partnerships.

Economically, Algeria is rich in natural resources, particularly oil and natural gas, which account for the majority of government revenues and export earnings. It is one of the largest natural gas exporters to Europe. However, the heavy reliance on hydrocarbons has also created vulnerabilities, with fluctuations in global oil prices directly impacting the economy. In recent years, the government has been attempting to diversify into agriculture, renewable energy, and tourism. Agriculture is important in the northern regions, producing wheat, barley, citrus fruits, olives, and dates, while traditional markets continue to thrive in rural areas.
