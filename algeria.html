🇩🇿 Algeria
Capital: Algiers

Algeria is the largest country in Africa, stretching across 2.38 million square kilometers. It borders the Mediterranean Sea to the north and is surrounded by Morocco, Tunisia, Libya, Mali, Niger, Mauritania, and Western Sahara. The north features fertile plains and the rugged Atlas Mountains, while the south is dominated by the Sahara Desert, which covers nearly 80% of the country. Algeria’s geography influences its settlement patterns: most people live along the coast, while the desert remains sparsely inhabited. Its varied landscapes support diverse ecosystems, from olive groves in the north to desert oases in the Sahara. Natural resources, including oil, gas, and minerals, are concentrated in the Sahara, making geography vital to Algeria’s economy.

Algeria has a population of over 45 million people, with most concentrated in coastal cities such as Algiers, Oran, and Constantine. The Sahara region has only a small share of the population, primarily in oasis towns. The society is predominantly Arab-Berber, reflecting centuries of cultural blending between indigenous Berbers and Arab settlers. Islam is central to Algerian life, with nearly all citizens being Muslim. Algeria also has a young demographic, with a large proportion under 30 years old, creating opportunities for innovation but also challenges in providing jobs, housing, and education. French, though not official, remains widely used in education, business, and government due to Algeria’s colonial past.

Algeria is a semi-presidential republic. The President serves as the head of state and commander of the armed forces, while the Prime Minister oversees day-to-day government operations. Algeria’s political system is based on a multi-party structure, though power has traditionally been centralized. The country is divided into 58 provinces, each with local administrations. While democracy and civil liberties have expanded since independence in 1962, Algeria continues to face political challenges, including calls for greater transparency, youth participation, and reduced corruption. National priorities include maintaining stability, economic diversification, and strengthening international relations, especially with Europe, Africa, and the Arab world.

Algeria’s economy is one of the largest in Africa and is heavily dependent on oil and natural gas, which account for more than 90% of export earnings. The Sahara Desert is rich in hydrocarbons, making Algeria a key energy supplier to Europe. Beyond energy, Algeria produces iron ore, phosphates, and other minerals. Agriculture thrives in northern regions, producing wheat, barley, olives, dates, and citrus fruits. The government has been working to diversify the economy through industrial projects, renewable energy development, and tourism. Challenges include unemployment, inflation, and dependence on global oil prices. Economic reforms aim to encourage private investment and reduce reliance on hydrocarbons.

Algeria’s culture reflects a rich blend of Arab, Berber, Islamic, and French influences. Traditional music such as Raï, which originated in Oran, is internationally known. Other popular styles include Chaabi and Andalusian music. Algerian cuisine is a highlight of its culture, with couscous considered the national dish, accompanied by tajines, grilled meats, and pastries flavored with honey and almonds. Literature and film have flourished, with Algerian authors and directors gaining international recognition. The country celebrates both Islamic and national holidays, and festivals often showcase traditional dances, crafts, and storytelling. Family life remains central to Algerian society, with strong values of hospitality and community.

Algeria experiences diverse climates due to its size and geography. The northern coastal region has a Mediterranean climate with mild, wet winters and hot, dry summers, making it the agricultural heartland. The Atlas Mountains create cooler temperatures and more rainfall in higher elevations. In contrast, the Sahara Desert has extreme conditions: scorching heat during the day, freezing nights, and minimal rainfall. Seasonal winds like the sirocco bring hot, dusty air from the desert into northern areas, affecting health and agriculture. Climate change poses new challenges, with rising temperatures and desertification threatening water supplies and farming productivity.

Algeria maintains one of the strongest militaries in Africa, composed of the Army, Navy, and Air Force. Its strategic priorities include protecting borders, counterterrorism, and maintaining regional stability. The military is equipped with modern aircraft, tanks, and naval vessels, often sourced from Russia and other partners. Algeria also participates in peacekeeping and counterterrorism operations across Africa. Domestically, the military plays a role in disaster relief and infrastructure development. Its strength ensures national sovereignty and enhances Algeria’s role as a regional power in the Mediterranean and North Africa.
